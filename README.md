## 🖌️ Materials Needed

Make sure you have all your supplies ready before starting:

- [ ] **Canvas:** Medium texture, landscape orientation (e.g., 18"x24").
- [ ] **Brushes:** 
  - Large flat brush (for background)
  - Medium filbert brush (for blending and waves)
  - Small round brush (for fine details and rain)
- [ ] **Tools:** Palette knife (optional, for heavy texture on the coat), Graphite pencil or charcoal for sketching.
- [ ] **Paints (Acrylic or Oil):**
  - Titanium White
  - Mars Black (or Payne's Gray)
  - Ultramarine Blue
  - Burnt Umber
  - Yellow Ochre
  - Cadmium Yellow (Medium or Light)

## 🛠️ Step-by-Step Guide

### Step 1: The Sketch and Composition
Before putting paint on the canvas, establish the composition. This piece relies on a center-weighted subject to convey a feeling of isolation against the vastness of nature.

1. Using a graphite pencil or watered-down gray paint, lightly draw a horizontal line across the middle of your canvas. This is your horizon line.
2. Sketch the basic outline of the figure in the exact center. Notice the shape: a triangular hood tapering down to a broader base.
3. Sketch the rectangular shapes of the boots. Keep it simple; don't worry about folds or details yet.

### Step 2: Blocking in the Stormy Sky and Sea
Establish the darkest, furthest elements of the painting. Work from back to front, top to bottom.

1. Mix a dark, stormy gray using Payne’s Gray, a touch of Blue, and White.
2. Using your large flat brush, apply this dark gray to the top half of the canvas using broad, sweeping, messy strokes to simulate turbulent clouds.
3. Establish your horizon line just above the center. Paint the distant ocean with a slightly darker, cooler gray, using strict horizontal strokes. Leave the bottom bare.

### Step 3: Blocking in the Wet Beach and Figure Silhouette
Establish the ground and the placement of the subject. It is crucial to get the silhouette right before adding thick paint.

1. Mix White, a tiny amount of Burnt Umber, and a touch of gray to create a flat, sandy taupe color. 
2. Paint the bottom half of the canvas using smooth, horizontal strokes.
3. In the center, paint the flat silhouette of the figure using a dull Yellow Ochre mixed with a tiny bit of brown. This acts as our shadow layer. 
4. Paint the boots using a very dark gray. *Do not add details yet.*
<img width="1408" height="736" alt="Generated Image March 29, 2026 - 3_02PM (2)" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/e6f39a3c-7c23-428a-b97f-739cfc401f4a" />


### Step 4: Developing the Crashing Waves
Add energy and drama to the background, pushing the stormy sky further back.

1. Load a medium brush (or palette knife) with mostly Titanium White and a tiny bit of light gray.
2. Apply this vigorously right along the horizon line, using upward, stippling, and diagonal strokes to simulate sea spray.
3. Allow the white paint to be thick and textured (impasto) so it stands out against the smooth, dark sky.

### Step 5: Adding Raindrops and Ground Reflections
Fully integrate the figure into the wet environment.

1. **The Reflection:** Mix a watery version of your Yellow Ochre. Drag this color straight down into the sand directly beneath the boots. Use a clean, dry brush to lightly sweep horizontally across those strokes to create a watery mirror effect. 
2. **The Rain:** Thin down some white/light gray paint with water or painting medium. Using a small round brush, flick or paint very thin, faint diagonal lines across the canvas.

### Step 6: Painting the Vibrant Raincoat (The Focal Point)
Transition the coat from a flat silhouette to a textured, glowing focal point.

1. Load your brush heavily with pure Cadmium Yellow and Titanium White. Do not over-mix; let them marble together.
2. Apply thick, expressive strokes (impasto) over your ochre silhouette. 
3. Follow the imaginary folds of the fabric—vertical strokes on the back, angled strokes around the hood. Allow the darker ochre underpainting to show through in the creases as shadows.
4. Use messy, vertical strokes at the bottom hem to make it look wind-blown.

### Step 7: Finishing Touches and Varnishing

1. **Evaluate Values:** Step back 10 feet. Does the bright yellow pop against the dark grays? Add thin, dark gray glazes to the top corners of the sky if it needs more contrast.
2. **Edge Control:** Ensure the edges of the raincoat are slightly blurred. A storm is wet and windy; this helps seat the figure inside the atmosphere.
3. **Varnishing:** 
   - *Acrylics:* Let dry for 24 hours. Apply a gloss or satin varnish.
   - *Oils:* Wait until completely dry to the touch (can take weeks). Apply a permanent protective gloss varnish to enhance the "wet" look of the rain and ocean.

## 💡 Tips for Success

> **🎨 Color Mixing Tip:** Never use pure black from the tube for your shadows or skies. Always mix it with a color (like Ultramarine Blue or Burnt Umber) to keep the painting looking rich and full of life.
> 
> **🖌️ Texture:** The success of this painting relies on contrast—not just in color, but in texture. Keep the sky smooth and thin, and make the waves and the yellow coat thick and heavily textured!
