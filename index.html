How to Paint a Stormy Seascape with a Figure A Step-by-Step Acrylic / Oil Painting Tutorial This tutorial will guide you through creating a moody, atmospheric seascape featuring a lone figure in a vibrant yellow raincoat. We will build the painting in layers, starting from dark, rough backgrounds and moving toward bright, textured focal points. Materials Needed: Canvas: Medium texture, landscape orientation. Paints (Acrylic or Oil): Titanium White, Mars Black (or Payne's Gray), Ultramarine Blue, Burnt Umber, Yellow Ochre, Cadmium Yellow (Medium or Light). Brushes: Large flat brush (for background), medium filbert brush (for blending), small round brush (for details), and a palette knife (optional for heavy texture). Step 1: Blocking in the Stormy Sky and Sea We begin by establishing the darkest, furthest elements of the painting. We work from back to front, top to bottom. Mix a dark, stormy gray using Payne’s Gray, a touch of Blue, and White. Using your large flat brush, apply this dark gray to the top half of the canvas using broad, sweeping, messy strokes to simulate turbulent clouds. Establish your horizon line just above the center of the canvas. Paint the distant ocean with a slightly darker, cooler gray, using strict horizontal strokes. Leave the bottom half of the canvas bare for now. ![alt text](https://storage.googleapis.com/chat-bot-external-assets/7b94998caec6c802bc0f7ba054ef673cdd833bc883656abf2df4cf0b2a59a72e.png) Step 2: Blocking in the Wet Beach and Figure Silhouette Next, we establish the ground and the placement of our subject. It is crucial to get the silhouette right before adding thick, bright paint. Mix White, a tiny amount of Burnt Umber (for warmth), and a touch of gray to create a flat, sandy taupe color. Paint the bottom half of the canvas using smooth, horizontal strokes to represent the wet beach. In the center of the canvas, paint the flat silhouette of the figure using a dull Yellow Ochre mixed with a tiny bit of brown. This acts as our shadow layer. Paint the boots using a very dark gray (almost black). Do not add any details, highlights, or textures yet. ![alt text](https://storage.googleapis.com/chat-bot-external-assets/a7522ff611a512686afad3c36c6418859e98e29a8a72ec22c9e74ce9aebf5f3e.png) Step 3: Developing the Crashing Waves Now we add energy and drama to the background, pushing the stormy sky further back. Load a medium brush or a palette knife with mostly Titanium White and a tiny bit of light gray. Apply this paint vigorously right along the horizon line, using upward, stippling, and diagonal strokes to simulate sea spray and crashing waves. Allow the white paint to be thick and textured (impasto) so it stands out against the smooth, dark sky. ![alt text](https://storage.googleapis.com/chat-bot-external-assets/cfd8d6725206dd65d5064ed34d4023793dfab567c9c0f99d9869ca8df981329c.png) Step 4: Adding Raindrops and Ground Reflections Before we finish the figure, we need to fully integrate them into this wet environment. The Reflection: Mix a watery version of your Yellow Ochre. Drag this color straight down into the sand directly beneath the boots. Use a clean, dry brush to lightly sweep horizontally across those strokes to create a watery, mirrored reflection. Do the same with dark gray beneath the boots. The Rain: Thin down some white/light gray paint with water or a painting medium. Using a small round brush, flick or paint very thin, faint diagonal lines across the canvas. Notice how the rain streaks cross over the dark sky, the waves, and the beach, tying the whole scene together. ![alt text](https://storage.googleapis.com/chat-bot-external-assets/bdc4377ff26b68eddd82a472c57f7dfd675665805afb9ba63683f2a0df8b502e.png) Step 5: Painting the Vibrant Raincoat (The Focal Point) Note: Look back to your original reference image for this final, crucial step. This is where your painting comes alive. The coat transitions from a flat silhouette to a textured, glowing focal point. Load your brush heavily with pure Cadmium Yellow and Titanium White. Do not over-mix them on your palette; let them marble together. Apply thick, expressive strokes (impasto) over your ochre silhouette. Follow the imaginary folds of the fabric—vertical strokes on the back, angled strokes around the hood and arms. Allow the darker ochre underpainting to show through in the creases to act as shadows. Use messy, vertical strokes at the bottom hem of the coat to make it look wind-blown, frayed, and heavy with water. Add a few stark white highlights to the shoulders and hood to show the wet material catching the light.
